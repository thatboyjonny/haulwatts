Tesla quoted me $3,000 for a new computer. The fix was a $10 subscription.

By Jonathan · 2022 Tesla Model Y Performance · Saga: 2024–2026 · Filed under: you have got to be kidding me

Two years ago, in 2024, the Bluetooth and WiFi on my 2022 Model Y Performance just... stopped. No warning, no error message worth anything — the car simply lost the ability to connect to the world. I did everything an owner can do: the scroll-wheel reboots, full vehicle resets, disconnecting the 12-volt battery, the whole owner's-forum liturgy. Nothing. So I took it to Tesla, and that's where this story turns into a two-year lesson in warranty timing, workarounds, and the most anticlimactic fix in automotive history.

The $3,000 quote and the warranty that missed by inches

Tesla's bumper-to-bumper warranty runs four years or 50,000 miles. My car was just over 50,000 when this happened — and per Tesla, the failure occurred right after coverage expired, so there was nothing they could do for free. Their diagnosis: the car needed a new computer. Their price: about $3,000. My answer: absolutely not. When I asked for other options, they offered exactly one, and it was a strange one — bring the car to a Tesla service center, and while it sat within their WiFi bubble, they could force software updates over the air.

Life in the bubble

So that became the routine: drive to the service center like a pilgrim, park in the bubble, receive updates. It worked, in the way that carrying water in your hands works. Then around September of last year, even that door closed — the updates had grown too big to push over the air, they told me. From that point on the car was frozen in time: no software updates, no Full Self-Driving updates, no map updates, no new games. And since this Model Y works in my Turo fleet, every booking came with an apology I got very tired of typing: hi, welcome, quick note — there's no Bluetooth or WiFi in this vehicle, sorry. A 2022 performance SUV with the connectivity of a 1998 Corolla.

My long-shot hope was hardware: Tesla has talked about upgrading Hardware 3 cars, and I figured if a retrofit ever came, it would solve my problem as a side effect. So I waited. And time, as it does, went on.

The fix nobody at the service center suggested

Then came the accident that solved it. A Turo guest recently swapped from the Model Y to the F-150 Lightning, and while sorting that out I noticed my Premium Connectivity subscription had lapsed — the payment had simply stopped going through at some point. On a whim, I paid for Premium Connectivity again, reset the car, and — voilà. Bluetooth: working. WiFi: working. I am not exaggerating what happened next: I pulled over and thanked God, out loud, in a parking lot. Two years. Then I got to work, connected the car to my phone's hotspot, and let it drink: every update it had missed, one after another, until it was fully current — FSD included.

The honest caveat: I can't prove the computer was never bad, and I can't prove the lapsed subscription caused the whole outage — maybe the reset did it, maybe some account-state gremlin cleared, maybe the stars aligned. What I can prove is this: a $9.99 subscription and a reset accomplished what a $3,000 computer replacement quote was supposed to. If your Tesla loses connectivity, check your subscription and account status and do a full reset before you approve a four-figure repair. It costs ten dollars to find out.

Perfect timing: FSD v14 Lite just dropped for cars like mine

Here's why getting current right now matters. My 2022 Model Y runs Hardware 3, and every HW3 Tesla on Earth has been frozen on FSD v12.6 since early 2025 while the newer HW4 cars ran off with v13 and v14. That drought just ended: on June 29, 2026 — days ago — Tesla began rolling out FSD v14 Lite to HW3 vehicles, firmware 2026.20.5.1, starting with early-access drivers and widening over the coming weeks. It's a distilled version of the HW4 v14 stack squeezed onto the older computer, bringing the new parking and unparking capabilities, arrival options, speed profiles, and smoother behavior — still a supervised, hands-on system, to be clear, but the first real FSD progress HW3 owners have seen in over a year. After two years of my car being frozen out of updates entirely, I went from software purgatory to the front of the line in one billing cycle. So now I wait for v14 Lite to reach my VIN — patiently, and with working Bluetooth.

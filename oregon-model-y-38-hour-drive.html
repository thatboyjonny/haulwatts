Road Trip · Log 003 · Buying Used

I flew to Oregon to buy a used Model Y, then drove it 2,000 miles home in 38 hours.

By Jonathan · 2021 Tesla Model Y Performance w/ FSD · Private-party purchase, 2022 · Eugene, OR → San Angelo, TX

In 2022, used Teslas hit a place the car market had never really been: some of them were selling for more used than they cost new. Waitlists were long, inventory was nothing, and if you wanted a specific build, you hunted for it. I was hunting for a Model Y Performance with Full Self-Driving already on it — and the deal I found was a 2021, private party, up in Eugene, Oregon. I was not in Oregon.

So I did the thing that sounds insane until you run the numbers: I bought a one-way plane ticket. We'd connected over text and worked out the details ahead of time; I flew out, met the seller, test drove the car, went through everything on it, and we did the transfer. An hour after landing in a state I'd never planned to visit, I owned a Tesla and a very long drive home.

Then the trip changed

The plan was a road trip. What I got was a family emergency back in Texas, which turned a leisurely drive home into a mission: roughly 2,000 miles, as fast as a battery allows. The final tally was about 38 hours in and around that car — call it 26 hours of driving and about 12 hours of charging. That charging number is the honest one nobody puts in the brochure: on a maximum-urgency EV road trip in 2022, roughly a third of your journey is spent parked at a Supercharger.

Here's the part that surprised me most, given the circumstances: even on a mission, that route is absurdly beautiful. Coming down through California, the coastline stretches were the kind of driving people buy sports cars to pretend they do. Then the landscape flips — Arizona and New Mexico canyon country, all that red rock and open sky. If I'd had the time the trip deserved, it would have been one of the great American drives. Instead I got the highlight reel at 75 mph, which is still better than most people's Tuesday.

It was also my first time road-tripping a Tesla, which came with its own education: the arrival estimate is an optimist. The navigation would promise 20 percent at the next charger and I'd roll in with a little less than that, every time. It wasn't a one-trip fluke either — the same pattern held on the family trip we took later that year, where we settled on a working rule: whatever the car promises, mentally subtract 5 to 10 points and plan around that.

I didn't stop at hotels. When I had to sleep, I folded the seats and slept in the back of the Model Y between charging sessions. Here's what I learned at two in the morning somewhere on that route: the Model Y's cargo area is flat enough to sleep in and hard enough to make you regret it. A proper camping mattress for the car — the kind I now know exists — would have transformed that trip. I did it on bare trunk floor and jacket-as-pillow. Learn from me.

The Supercharger roulette rule: some charging stops were genuinely pleasant — food nearby, well-lit, easy. Others were in strange, awkward corners of nowhere: the back of a dark lot, a location that made no sense, nothing open for miles. In 2022 you found out which kind you'd drawn only when you arrived. Checking what's actually at a charger — not just that it exists — is now the first thing I do when planning any EV route.

Full Self-Driving, 2022 edition

The car had FSD, and on a 2,000-mile forced march, I used it plenty. It helped — hours of interstate are exactly what driver assistance is for — but the 2022 version had its quirks, and the one that still makes me laugh was the sunglasses. The cabin camera watches your eyes to make sure you're paying attention, and mine seemed personally offended by my sunglasses, nagging me on bright highway stretches where sunglasses are, you know, the whole point. That was four years ago; the software has been through a lot of versions since, and I'd expect a different experience today. But it's a good reminder that "the car drives itself" has always come with an asterisk, and the asterisk was sometimes wearing Ray-Bans.

The accessories that sealed the deal (and the mods that followed)

One underrated perk of buying private party: the seller threw in a small pile of accessories with the car. All-weather mats for the cabin plus liners for the frunk and trunk — exactly what you want with kids and dogs in your future — and, the one that genuinely surprised me, a replacement dash panel. I had no idea you could swap out the Model Y's dash trim until a stranger in Eugene handed me a spare one. Consider that my introduction to the Tesla accessory rabbit hole.

Because once you're in the rabbit hole, you keep digging. Over the time I owned that car I ended up doing a proper round of upgrades: swapped the center console's USB-A ports for USB-C like the newer builds, added a rear touchscreen for the back seats the way the new Model Ys come from the factory, and fitted puddle lights that project the Tesla logo onto the ground when a door opens — pure vanity, zero regrets. I also had the windows tinted to 20 percent all around, which in Texas sun is less a mod than a survival requirement. And if the sun still finds you, the cheapest fix on the list: a windshield sun reflector that folds behind the glass and bounces the heat back out. Twenty-something dollars, and the cabin stops being an oven.

Was flying to buy a car worth it?

Completely — with caveats. At 2022 prices, the right used Tesla in another state could beat anything local by thousands, even after a one-way flight. The private-party process itself was smooth: connect by text, verify everything in person, test drive before money moves. What I'd tell anyone repeating it: build your charging route home before you commit to the car, assume a third of your travel time will be charging, and if there is any chance you sleep in the vehicle, spend the fifty bucks on a mattress made for it. The car made it. I made it. My back filed a complaint.

That Model Y did its tour of duty and eventually went back to the well — traded in toward the Hummer EV Omega SUV that anchors this site today. But it earned its place in the fleet history the hard way: 2,000 miles, 38 hours, zero hotels, one very annoyed cabin camera.

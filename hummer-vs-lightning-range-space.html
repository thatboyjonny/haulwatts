Owner Comparison · Log 005 · Range & Space

Hummer EV SUV vs F-150 Lightning: which gauge lies, and which back seat wins.

By Jonathan · 2024 Hummer EV Omega SUV + 2023 Lightning Lariat ER · Both owner-purchased, both daily driven

Most Hummer-versus-Lightning comparisons are written by someone who had each truck for a week. Both of these live in my driveway, get driven by the same family, haul the same kids and the same two corgis. This isn't the full head-to-head — that'll build over many logs — but it covers the two things buyers ask me about most: whose range gauge tells the truth, and which one your family actually wants to sit in.

Range honesty: one flatters, one sandbags

Put the estimated range next to what the trucks actually deliver and you get two opposite personalities. The Lightning is the optimist — the number on the screen runs ahead of reality, and you learn to discount it. If you've read the −20°F trip log, you've seen the extreme version, but the pattern shows up in normal weather too: the Lightning tells you what it hopes will happen.

For context, the factory numbers: the Lightning Lariat Extended Range is rated at 320 miles EPA, while the Omega Edition SUV is rated at 298 — lower than the standard 3X's 314 because the Omega ships with the Extreme Off-Road Package and 35-inch mud terrain tires, which tax efficiency. So on paper the Ford wins by 22 miles.

The Hummer is the opposite: it sandbags. In my ownership, its real-world range consistently comes in better than what the gauge promises. You leave with a conservative number and arrive with miles you didn't expect to have. As a daily-driving experience, I'll take the pessimist every time — a truck that underpromises never strands you, and there's something deeply funny about the 9,000-pound monster on mud terrain tires being the humble one. Which makes the paper gap misleading: rate the Lightning by what it delivers and the Hummer by what it delivers, and that 22-mile factory advantage shrinks — or flips — in the real world.

The back seat: not close

Here's the result that surprises people at this price gap: for family space, the Lightning wins, and it isn't close — and the spec sheet backs up what the booster seats told us. The Lightning's rear legroom measures about 43.6 inches; the Hummer's is 38.9. That's nearly five inches, which in kid-hauling terms is the difference between boosters-stay-in comfort and knees-on-seatbacks. And here's the part I said from experience that turned out to be generous when I checked the numbers: I claimed the Hummer's back seat was barely bigger than a Tesla Model Y's. On paper, the Model Y actually has more rear legroom — about 40.5 inches to the Hummer's 38.9. A compact crossover out-legrooms the Death Star. The width story is the same: the Lightning carries roughly 2.8 more inches of front shoulder room and 2 more in back, so its cabin actually delivers what the exterior design promises. The Hummer's exterior writes checks its interior doesn't fully cash.

The parent details that decide it: the Lightning's rear cup holders sit right in the middle where kids can actually reach them — small thing, daily win. And there's storage under the rear seat, a whole hidden bin layer the Hummer simply doesn't offer. When every seat has a child and every child has stuff, under-seat storage is worth more than a spec-sheet horsepower number.

Up front, the Hummer takes one back

Slide into the driver's seat and my vote flips. The Hummer's driving space is the one I personally prefer — the position, the room around you, and the headspace are excellent, helped by that glass sky overhead. It feels like commanding something. The Lightning's cab is the more familiar F-150 experience, and its big roof glass makes it airy too, but as a place to drive from, the Hummer wins my seat time.

Quirks corner: the window situation

One Hummer annoyance that costs nothing to fix and yet here we are: the windows aren't one-touch automatic like practically every other vehicle at any price. Auto roll-down works — tap and it drops — but rolling up means holding the switch the whole way like it's 2004. In a six-figure technology flagship, that's a strange corner to cut. What redeems the Hummer's glass story is the SUV's party trick: the rear tailgate glass powers down, old-school-SUV style. Dogs love it, drive-through pickups love it, and it's the kind of feature that makes you forgive the window switches. Almost.

The space verdict

If the question is which truck serves the family, the winner is definitely the Lightning — the rear room, the reachable cup holders, the under-seat storage, the glass roof for everyone to enjoy. If the question is which one I'd rather be behind the wheel of, the Hummer. That split is exactly why both are still in the driveway, and why this comparison will keep growing a category at a time: charging behavior, software, road trips, and winters are all coming in future logs. Same trucks, same driver, real answers.

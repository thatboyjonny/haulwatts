This one isn't a memory — I finished this trip hours before writing it up, and every number below comes straight from the charge logs. The mission: Shenandoah, Texas (just north of Houston) to Colorado Springs, roughly a thousand miles, with a trailer on the hitch the whole way. Towing is the EV stress test everyone asks about, so here's the complete, unedited answer: every stop, every kilowatt-hour, and the one charging station that held my truck hostage in the middle of the night.

The towing math, stated plainly

Hang a trailer on an electric truck and the range math gets simple and brutal: charging to my usual 80 percent bought roughly 160 miles of real towing range — sometimes. That's the working number I planned around, and the session logs back it up: at one overnight 100 percent charge, the truck estimated 174 miles total. Against a 320-mile EPA rating, towing costs you about half, which matches what every honest EV tower will tell you and what no brochure will. In practice that means a charging stop every 100 to 130 miles, which is why a thousand-mile trip produced fourteen sessions and nearly ten and three-quarter hours plugged in.

The full charge log

I'm publishing the whole thing because this is the data I wish existed before my first towing trip. Fourteen stops, four different charging networks — the NACS adapter earned its keep again, unlocking Tesla Superchargers (including a Magic Dock site) alongside the CCS networks. Some routes basically require it now, and mixing networks is simply how you tow across the middle of America.

A few patterns worth pulling out of the table. The overnight legs were the strategy: charging at midnight and 2 AM means empty stalls, full speed, and no waiting behind anyone — when you need fourteen sessions, stall availability becomes trip planning. The Sayre Flying J session tells the towing truth in one line: 11 to 100 percent took two hours, because charging past 80 crawls, but when the next leg needs every mile, you eat the taper. And stop number three is an old friend — an H-E-B with charging, the same grocery-store-charging combo I fell in love with on the Pensacola family trip. Texas keeps proving chargers belong at grocery stores.

Trinidad, Colorado: the charger that wouldn't let go

Everything ran smooth — genuinely smooth, fourteen hundred miles of towing smooth — until Trinidad, Colorado, after midnight, at an Electrify America station. The charge itself was fine: 51 to 80 percent, twenty easy minutes. Then I went to unplug, and the connector would not release from the truck. Not stuck like "jiggle it" stuck. Stuck like it lives here now.

I tried everything in the book and then the whole book again: the manual latch release, restarting the truck, calling the charging network's service line, every sequence and combination I could think of, in a parking lot in the small hours. This went on for roughly five hours. What finally worked, on something like the fourth full attempt at the latch procedure, was the manual release finally letting the latch drop enough to pull the connector free. No tow truck, no locksmith heroics — just stubbornness, repetition, and eventually the latch giving up before I did.

Learn the latch release before you need it: every EV has a manual charge-port release — in the Lightning's case it's reachable from inside the frunk area — and the time to find yours is in your driveway, not at 1 AM in Trinidad. Know where it is, know how it feels when it works, and know that sometimes the fix is the same procedure attempted with more patience than the situation deserves. If I hadn't known the truck had a manual release at all, that five-hour delay becomes a next-day service call.

The verdict on towing cross-country

Would I tow a thousand miles electric again? I literally just did, and the answer is yes — with eyes open. The real cost isn't range anxiety, it's rhythm: stops every hundred-and-change miles, ten-plus cumulative hours of charging, and a route built around where the plugs are rather than where you'd naturally stop. The adapter that opens Tesla's network makes the whole thing dramatically more workable, the overnight-charging strategy beats fighting for stalls at 5 PM, and the one genuine failure in fourteen sessions wasn't the truck or the battery — it was a latch. The math is honest and the trip is doable. Just pack patience, and know where your manual release lives.

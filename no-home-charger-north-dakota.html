Winter · Log 002 · Apartment EV Life

I owned an F-150 Lightning for a year with no home charger. In North Dakota.

By Jonathan · 2023 F-150 Lightning Lariat Extended Range · Owner-purchased · Grand Forks, ND

Every article about EV ownership starts with the same assumption: you have a garage, and in that garage is a Level 2 charger. For a year in Grand Forks, North Dakota, I had neither. I lived alone in an apartment with no charger anywhere on the property, in a place where winter regularly means zero degrees and sometimes means thirty below. This is what that year actually looked like — the worst-case version of apartment EV ownership, in the worst-case climate, with a truck that has one of the biggest batteries you can buy.

Life on a 110-volt outlet

My charging plan at home was a regular 110V wall outlet — the same one you'd plug a toaster into. Plug the Lightning in overnight, or over a whole weekend, and just leave it. The math on why that requires patience: a standard outlet delivers a bit over 1 kilowatt to the truck. The Extended Range Lightning carries a 131 kWh usable pack. Charging that from low to full on 110V takes the better part of four to five days. Overnight, you're gaining maybe 20 to 30 miles of range in decent weather — and in deep cold, a chunk of that trickle goes to keeping the battery itself from freezing instead of into miles.

So the strategy became lifestyle, not just charging. If I didn't need the truck, it stayed plugged in and I rode my bike instead. Summer, easy call. Winter, I'd still ride if it was tolerable — but when Grand Forks decided it was zero degrees with that wet, biting cold, the bike stayed home too. The truck banked miles all week; I spent them only when it mattered.

Hillsboro, North Dakota: the town the battery chose

Here's the thing nobody tells you about EV ownership without a home charger: you develop relationships with places you'd otherwise never stop. Mine was Hillsboro, North Dakota — a small town on I-29 roughly halfway between Grand Forks and Fargo that I would have driven past for the rest of my life without a thought. Hillsboro had a ChargePoint DC fast charger. Sixty kilowatts. And it was free.

Free 60 kW fast charging is the kind of thing you can't beat, and I used it frequently. At typical public fast-charging prices, a good session into that 131 kWh pack is $30 to $45 of electricity — Hillsboro handed it over for nothing, right next to a gas station. For most of my stint there was even a Subway beside it, which is about the perfect charging-stop amenity. Then, around eight months in, the Subway closed down, and I'm still a little bitter about it. The gas station soldiered on. So did I.

The apartment-EV survival rule I learned: you don't need a home charger — you need a routine. One reliable free or cheap DC charger on a route you already drive is worth more than three fancy ones across town. Scout it before you buy the truck, not after.

The I-29 wind tax

My regular long haul was I-29 south to Fargo, about 80 miles, to catch flights to Denver. On a map it's a straight, flat, easy drive. In reality, that flat is the problem: there's nothing out there to slow the wind down, and in winter it howls across the plains straight into your consumption numbers. Headwind at highway speed is a range tax the EPA never met — pair a hard plains wind with negative twenty or negative thirty degrees, and the truck's range versus its sticker rating was, to put it politely, terrible. I've written up exactly how bad the cold alone gets in Log 001; add wind and it's worse. You learn to leave with more buffer than feels reasonable, because out there the math is never in your favor.

The adapter that saved the airport run

Fargo's airport had no regular EV charging when I was flying out of it — but there was a Tesla charger. That's where the NACS adapter earned its keep: plug adapter into Tesla connector, plug truck into adapter, walk into the terminal. Without it, an airport trip meant coming home to a nearly empty truck and a four-day date with the 110V outlet. With it, the Lightning drank from Tesla hardware like it was born to. If you own a non-Tesla EV in a rural area, the adapter isn't an accessory — it roughly doubles your usable charging map, because in a lot of small-town America, Tesla got there first.

Would I recommend it?

Owning an EV truck with no home charger, in one of the coldest metros in America, is playable — I'm proof — but it demands three things: a trickle outlet you can monopolize, one anchor fast charger you trust (thank you, Hillsboro), and the discipline to treat range like a budget instead of a gauge. If you have all three, apartment EV life works. If you have none, buy the charger situation before you buy the truck. And if you're ever on I-29 and see a Lightning at that ChargePoint — pour one out for the Subway.
